Appendix · Technical Q&A — 01

Q1.Why a 2.75 threshold for the risk label?

In one paragraph

The risk label combines a trust score (NPS logic) with a friction index (Kano weights). We needed a cut-off to split customers into at-risk (Y=1) and stable (Y=0). We chose 2.75 because it splits the base almost exactly in half — the ideal balance for a model to learn from — and because it mathematically guarantees that no single complaint can flag a customer alone. Risk always requires more than one signal of friction.

In detail
risk_score = NPS_points(trust) + Kano_flight_index(reasons)
Y = 1 if risk_score ≥ 2.75  ·  Y = 0 otherwise
Threshold% at-riskReading
2.0067%Too many positives — unusable
2.75 (chosen)50% exactPerfect class balance + operational reading
3.0046%Starts discarding useful signal

Since the max weight of any single reason is 2.0 (< 2.75), no individual reason crosses the threshold by itself — preserving the "more than one signal" design principle.

Appendix · Technical Q&A — 02

Q2.The SHAP formula — and how it works

In one paragraph

A model predicts, but on its own it won't tell you why it flagged someone — it's a black box. SHAP opens that box: for every customer, it tells you which variable weighed most in their prediction. It turns a risk number into a reason the business can act on.

It's what revealed our biggest finding: what most predicts churn risk isn't bad experience or complaints — it's fintech awareness, how actively the customer is shopping around.

In detail

SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) is rooted in Shapley values from game theory. It distributes the gap between a prediction and the base (average) prediction across the features:

f(x) = φ₀ + Σ φᵢ
φᵢ = contribution (signed) of feature i  ·  φ₀ = base value

Per customer we know how much each variable pushed the score toward risk or stable; aggregating the mean |SHAP| over the holdout gives global feature importance.

Why it matters here: the winning model is logistic regression (already interpretable via coefficients), but SHAP gives a truer read of real impact considering each feature's distribution — and produces a reason tag per customer that feeds the retention playbooks.

Appendix · Technical Q&A — 03

Q3.The 2.75 threshold vs. the 0.44 threshold

In one paragraph

These are two different cut-offs at two different stages, easy to confuse. 2.75 builds the label — it decides who counts as at-risk in the first place. 0.44 is the decision threshold — once the trained model outputs a probability, it decides when to call that probability "risk."

One creates the ground truth; the other reads the model's output.

In detail
2.75 — Label0.44 — Decision
StageBefore trainingAfter training
Applies torisk_score (NPS+Kano)P(Y=1) from model
PurposeDefine Y (ground truth)Convert probability → class
Tuned for50/50 class balanceMax F1 (recall ↑)

The 0.44 cut (vs. the default 0.50) is slightly more aggressive — it raised recall from 0.47 to 0.63 at the cost of some precision. A deliberate business call: don't let churners slip through.

Appendix · Technical Q&A — 04

Q4.How was k = 3 chosen for the archetypes?

In one paragraph

We grouped customers by similar behavior (clustering) into three archetypes. We chose three because it's the number that yields distinct, actionable groups — fewer segments nothing useful; more and you can't design a different strategy for each.

3stable, actionable archetypes (n ≥ 73)
In detail

K-Means on profile + behavior features (never the ones defining Y). We swept K from 2 to 8 measuring silhouette (how clean the group boundaries are):

KSilhouetteVerdict
40.131Wins by a hair — but isolates a 1-person cluster (outlier)
3 (chosen)0.128Near-identical score, three stable groups (n≥73)

Rule of thumb: the metric guides, but a cluster of size 1 is never a business segment. K=3 trades a negligible 0.003 in silhouette for three groups you can actually build strategy around.

Appendix · Technical Q&A — 05

Q5.Weight calibration — why theoretical, not statistical?

In one paragraph

The Kano model says something common-sense: not everything that annoys a customer weighs the same. Being charged unfair fees (a serious failure) isn't the same as wishing for better rewards (a nice-to-have). We assigned each switch reason a weight — severe failures weigh a lot, wishes weigh little — and summed them into a Flight Index per customer.

In detail — Kano weights
Kano categoryWeight
Must-Be (breakdown)2.0
Financial performance1.5
Operational performance1.25 ← tuned
Delighter (aspirational)0.8
External influence0.5
Loyalty / indifferent0.0

Why 1.25, not 1.0: so "bad app + bad service" sums to 2.5 and crosses the threshold when paired with any other signal. Why theoretical, not statistical: Cronbach's alpha came out at 0.20 (≪ 0.7) — the reasons don't form a coherent scale, so statistical weights would be false rigor. Kano weights are interpretable, stable and defensible.

Appendix · Technical Q&A — 06

Q6.Other questions worth having ready

If Y is synthetic, how do we predict churn?

We're not predicting real churn yet — we predict our own risk definition, built on solid criteria. It validates the methodology (scoring framework, signal independence, anti-leakage discipline), not predictive power against real attrition. Hence "behavioral risk signal," not "churn prediction." Phase 2 connects these signals to observed churn.

Why anti-leakage limits the score

We hide trust_level and switch_reasons (the variables that define Y) from the features X. Leaving them in would let the model reconstruct the formula and hit ROC-AUC ≈ 1.0 with zero real predictive power. The 0.66 ceiling is honest by design.

Why logistic regression beat RF / XGBoost
ModelROC-AUCF1
Logistic Reg. (winner)0.700.65
Random Forest0.680.63
XGBoost0.630.60

With only 240 training examples, complex models overfit — they memorize instead of generalize. At this data size simplicity wins, and logistic regression is interpretable: every prediction can be explained.

Rate vs. share of churn

Risk rate = % flagged Y=1 within a group. Share = fraction of all at-risk customers a group contributes. That's why 74+44+40 doesn't sum to 100 (three group averages), but 36+34+30 does (shares of total churn).
